December 4, Morning

The night was serene, even the machine-guns from the up-hill suburbs kept pregnant silence.

The previous day I visited Department Store to pick anything you can call a birthday present for a ten-year-old girl called Roozahna.
A sole pair of customers were hanging around in the murky void of the toy-department on top of two flights of stairs—Daddy in an effort to distract his kid from the present snafu mess of things.
The saleswoman sullenly paraded on the counter samples of plastic playthings taken randomly from the sets of their replicated likes invariably stuffing shelves at any department store of any Soviet city in any year after year.
'Anything else, jahnaJAHNA —
     Armenian term of endearment meaning something like "sweet baby". ?' asked Daddy.
There was no answer just a gaze from the boy's glum eyes at so generous yet useless deathbed sweepstake.

<!--When rubbing shoulders with the Grim Reaper you become a spendthrift of a customer.-->

Certain streaks of extravagancy cropped up even in Maxim, the Chief Editor of the only local newspaper.
'To stick it out down here, to see it through thick and thin is the uniquest opportunity for a journalist,' proclaimed he stately promenading to and fro in front of his subordinate gents—Wagrum and Lenic—sitting at attention at their office desks with eager beavers' looks.
And—as if adding an assertive ring to the delivered maxim—he jingled his regal bunch of keys dangling from his fatty hands clasped in the accustomed position over his mighty butt.

My backache keeps sticking around yet, and the shortness of Lydia's sofa makes me feel it even in sleep.
Yesterday I combed her bookshelves to be rewarded with a welcome catch—THE BHAGAVAT-GITA (in Russian)—and now look forward to a yummy evening pouring over it on that too short sofa of hers.

The same day, Evening.

It's hard to distinguish whether we are having a snowy rain or rainy snow outdoors. The tap in our kitchen yields a faint trickle but yields.

In the morning I had one more job interview with Arcadic, the of head the Russian section (getting bold).

Awoiding eye-contact he trotted out that their periodical was in need of my professional skills and in a week they'd give me the job of a renderer after seeing to the necessary staff-reshuffle and unavoidable managerial chicanery to hollow out the vacancy in their institution.
No one can accelerate the process, you know.

From my present standpoint (which invariably is here and now) it looks like a pretty far off affair in the future (but practice your patience, pal, when jobless).

In the afternoon Sat sent me on mission to fetch a jar of milk from the Milk Factory. On coming there I could find neither Valyo, nor any chap I knew.

To skip taking the empty jar back and then bringing it with me again for a better timed filling I just stashed it away, together with the bag, in a quite quiet nook.

On the second floor landing of the Administration Block with the door eternally ajar to the dark corridor on the right I hung the bag up on the other side of the door.

Not a chance of someone ever nosing it out. Eternity handlers are too rare a specimen in this cut-and-running world. Unseen and undisturbed will the bagged jar hang up on that unvexed door handle till my next visitation.

Later in the day Nerses, Lydia's husband, having arrived from his native village of Hnushinak, tap-tapped on the matt-glassed pane of the communicational window in our one-but-spacy-room flat. (When you open the window you can talk or pass things through the grates to a person standing right in front of it on the sidewalk. The other two windows in the room are just nailed up).

He was looking for the key of their house.

Oh, sure, here you are!

His coming into town brought to a close my watchmanship in their dwelling for security reasons.

Fare thee well, the BHAGAVAT-GITA, and thee as well, O, Procrustus' sofa!

My backache faithfully sticks around, however, this evening I don't have to check its loyalty; the Providence has benevolently seen to the kitchen tap trickling in about two pails of water before it stopped.
